Advanced Dementia Care Toolkit

A gentle collection of ideas, approaches, and practical tools to support people living with advanced dementia and the carers who walk alongside them. You don’t need to use everything – just choose what feels right for you, the person, and the moment.

This toolkit offers general support ideas and does not replace clinical advice or care plans. Always follow guidance from your healthcare team and local policies.

Communication tools Validation · Connection

Communication in advanced dementia is less about getting the “right” words and more about helping the person feel safe, heard, and understood.

  • Lead with feelings: respond to the emotion behind the words, even if details don’t fully make sense.
  • Validate, don’t argue: “It sounds like you’re worried about…” instead of “That’s not happening.”
  • Soft, slow, simple: one short sentence at a time, with pauses.
  • Warm body language: gentle tone, relaxed posture, eye level where possible.
  • Use familiar anchors: names of loved ones, familiar places, favourite routines.
  • If stuck, offer choices: “Would you like tea or water?” is often easier than open questions.
Sensory & comfort tools Soothing · Grounding

Sensory support can help someone feel calmer, safer, and more connected to the world around them.

  • Touch: soft blankets, cushions, hand massage if welcomed, lap blankets.
  • Smell: gentle, familiar scents, avoiding anything overpowering.
  • Sound: familiar music, nature sounds, quiet background radio.
  • Vision: calming photos, simple pictures, avoiding visual clutter.
  • Fidget items: textured fabrics, twiddle muffs, soft items to hold.
Environment support Safe · Familiar

Small changes in the environment can reduce confusion and make everyday life feel more manageable.

  • Lighting: avoid harsh glare and deep shadows.
  • Contrast: make key objects easier to see.
  • Reduce clutter: fewer objects can mean less confusion.
  • Familiar objects: treasured items can soothe.
  • Orientation cues: gentle signage if helpful.
  • Quiet spaces: a calmer place if overwhelmed.
Meaningful moments & activities Connection · Identity

Activities don’t have to be big. Small, familiar moments can be deeply meaningful.

  • Reminiscence: use prompts around school, holidays, music, food, and home.
  • Music: familiar songs can unlock comfort.
  • Life story: photo albums and memory boxes.
  • Simple tasks: folding towels, pairing socks, or sorting familiar items.
  • Creative moments: colouring, simple art, or gentle crafts.
  • Nature: window watching, fresh air, birds, sky, and seasonal changes.
Distress & agitation toolkit Calm · Reassure

When someone is distressed, the priority is to help them feel safe again, not to win an argument or correct facts.

  • Stay calm: slower movements and softer voice.
  • Join their reality: respond to feelings rather than correcting.
  • Reduce stimulation: lower noise and simplify the space.
  • Gentle reassurance: repeat simple soothing phrases.
  • Comfort focus: blanket, photo, drink, music.
  • Notice triggers: patterns often show in your journal.
Carer wellbeing You matter too

Caring is emotional, demanding work. Your wellbeing is not a luxury, it is vital.

  • Small pauses: 1 to 2 minutes can reset your nervous system.
  • Self-compassion: “I’m doing my best in a hard situation.”
  • Reach out: friend, family, support group.
  • Notice the good: write the small wins down.
  • Ask for help: sharing the load is strength.
  • Rest when you can: even imperfect rest helps.

Dementia Info Library

A gentle, plain-language library to help you understand dementia and everyday care. You don’t need to remember everything, dip in and out, and come back whenever you need.

This is general guidance and not a medical diagnosis. Always follow advice from your GP, specialist teams, and local services.

1. What is dementia? Understanding the basics

Dementia is not one single disease. It is a word used to describe a group of symptoms caused by changes in the brain. These changes can affect memory, thinking, problem-solving, mood, communication, and behaviour.

  • Different types: Alzheimer’s disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, and others.
  • It is progressive: it often changes over time, but the journey looks different for each person.
  • It affects everyday life: remembering, planning, following conversations, finding words, recognising people.
  • Behaviour changes: distress, confusion, or repeated questions are often part of the condition, not “bad behaviour”.
  • The person is still there: dementia changes abilities, but feelings, needs, and identity still matter deeply.
The Memory & Emotional Bookcase Analogy

Imagine the brain as a tall bookcase filled with books. Each book represents a memory.

The top shelves hold the newest memories, such as recent conversations or what happened earlier today. These are often the first to wobble, so the newer memory books may fall first.

The lower shelves hold older, deeper memories, such as childhood, school days, early jobs, and long-ago routines. These books are often stored more securely and may stay much longer.

This is why someone may forget what happened a few minutes ago, but clearly remember stories from decades ago.

💛 The feeling stays even when the memory fades

Even when a memory book falls, the emotion attached to it can remain. A person may:

  • Forget you visited earlier, but still feel comforted.
  • Forget what frightened them, but still feel anxious.
  • Forget a conversation, but still respond to the tone of your voice.

Feelings often last longer than facts.

🌼 What this means for carers

  • Focus on how you make the person feel, not what they remember.
  • Calmness, warmth, and reassurance have lasting emotional impact.
  • Correcting or arguing rarely helps. Kindness does.
  • Familiar voices, routines, and faces help steady the bookcase.

Your kindness creates emotional safety even when the memory of the moment is gone.

2. Communication made easier Talking · Listening

Dementia can make it harder to find words, follow conversations, or understand complex information. Small changes can make a big difference.

  • Keep it simple: one short idea at a time, with pauses.
  • Use names and context: “It’s Odette,” rather than “It’s me.”
  • Show as well as tell: point, gesture, or demonstrate when you can.
  • Repeat calmly: same gentle tone, no pressure.
  • Join their reality: respond to feelings instead of correcting facts.
  • Reduce background noise: it helps the brain focus.
3. Behaviour = communication Looking beyond the surface

Behaviour changes are often a way of communicating needs when words are difficult. Try asking: “What might they be trying to tell us?”

  • Agitation or anger: pain, fear, boredom, frustration, or feeling unsafe.
  • Walking or wandering: habit, searching, restlessness, or old routines.
  • “I want to go home”: often means wanting safety and familiarity.
  • Repeating questions: anxiety and reassurance-seeking.
  • Withdrawal: tiredness, overwhelm, low mood, or confusion.

If behaviour changes suddenly, consider pain, infection, constipation, urinary issues, or medication effects.

4. Sundowning & evening restlessness Late afternoon · Evenings

Some people become more confused or unsettled later in the day. This is sometimes called sundowning.

  • Why it may happen: tiredness, changing light, hunger, thirst, or overwhelm.
  • Soften the evening: quieter, calmer, cosier.
  • Gentle routine: drink, snack, toilet, soothing activity.
  • Reassure: “You’re safe, I’m here with you.”
5. Eating, drinking & hydration Food · Fluids

Dementia can change appetite and recognition of food and drink. Gentle adjustments can help.

  • Little and often can work better than large meals.
  • Favourites are okay, even repetitive ones.
  • Colour contrast helps food and cups stand out.
  • Hydration helpers: squash, tea, milky drinks, jelly, fruit.
  • Get advice if coughing, choking, or sudden weight loss appears.
6. Sleep & night waking Night-time support

Sleep can change. People may nap in the day or wake confused about time.

  • Daytime light and movement can support night sleep.
  • Wind-down routine: calm activities, lower stimulation.
  • Safety: night lights, clear paths, familiar objects.
  • Seek advice if changes are sudden or severe.
7. Personal care without distress Washing · Dressing

Personal care can feel embarrassing or frightening. Keep dignity at the centre.

  • Prepare first so it feels smooth and unhurried.
  • Short steps: “Now we’ll wash your hands.”
  • Offer choices rather than open questions.
  • Pause if distressed and try again later if safe.
  • Familiar products can make care feel safer.
8. Pain, health & medical needs Spotting the signs

Some people can’t say they’re in pain. Behaviour changes can be a clue.

  • Look for signs: grimacing, guarding, changes in walking, calling out.
  • Check basics: constipation, UTI, dehydration, medication effects.
  • Keep notes to share with the GP or care team.
9. Supporting emotional wellbeing Feelings · Comfort

Emotional support matters as much as physical care.

  • Validate feelings: “That sounds really hard.”
  • Small comforts: blankets, photos, music, familiar routines.
  • Connection: sitting together, eye contact, gentle presence.
  • Reduce overload: offer calmer spaces.
10. What helps you as a carer You are important

You are not a machine. Your wellbeing matters too.

  • Notice your needs such as food, rest, pain, and emotions.
  • Small pockets of rest count.
  • Talk to someone you trust or a support group.
  • Accept help when it’s offered.
  • Self-kindness: “I’m doing my best.”
Crisis Help & Support

If you’re feeling overwhelmed, unsafe, or like you can’t cope right now, you don’t have to carry it alone. This page is here for the hard moments.

If someone is in immediate danger Act now

Call 999 in the UK if there is immediate danger, a serious injury, or you feel someone may harm themselves or others.

Urgent medical advice, non-emergency Today

A grounding reset, 60 seconds Right now

1) Breathe in for 4, then out for 6. Repeat twice.

2) Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can feel, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, and 1 you can taste.

3) Say: “This is a hard moment. I am not alone. I can get support.”
